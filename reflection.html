Overall Capstone Reflection

A reflective narrative about how the quarter changed the way I work

This page focuses on what the capstone taught me about my habits, decision-making, and growth during the quarter rather than repeating the technical artifact discussion.

Starting with ambition instead of structure

I chose Ozora because I wanted a capstone that felt substantial enough to represent both product thinking and software engineering. At the start of the quarter, that ambition was helpful because it gave me energy and a strong sense that the project mattered. At the same time, I learned quickly that ambition without structure can create confusion. It is easy to say a project is about improving the dining experience, but it is much harder to define which parts of that experience the system actually needs to solve and which parts are only ideas that sound exciting.

The earliest phase of the capstone taught me that I tend to begin with a broad vision and only later force myself to narrow it into decisions. That pattern is useful for creativity, but it can also slow progress when a project depends on clear scope. I had to learn to move from a large concept into smaller commitments so the work could become visible, testable, and explainable.

Learning to revise work that already felt decided

One of the most important lessons of the quarter was becoming more willing to revise work that I had already mentally labeled as finished. Early decisions about layout, project framing, and system organization often felt settled when I first made them. Once feedback and implementation pressure were added, those decisions became less stable. I had to accept that changing direction was not a sign that the first attempt failed. It was part of doing the work seriously.

That shift mattered because it changed how I responded to criticism. Instead of defending the first version of an idea, I became more interested in what the revision was teaching me. The capstone made me more comfortable with the reality that software projects evolve through friction. The project improved when I treated revision as progress rather than as a setback.

Consistency mattered more than bursts of motivation

I also learned that motivation alone is not reliable enough to carry a quarter-long project. There were moments when the work felt exciting and moved quickly, but there were also stretches where the project felt heavy because it demanded writing, revision, and explanation in addition to technical work. In those stretches, the capstone depended more on consistency than inspiration.

That was a useful lesson about my own working habits. I work best when I break a large project into pieces that are small enough to finish and review. When I did that, the project felt manageable. When I tried to hold too many goals in my head at once, I lost momentum. The quarter showed me that sustainability is not just about endurance; it is about designing a process that makes continued work possible.

Explaining the work made the work clearer

Another major takeaway came from the need to explain the project to different audiences. The user page, artifact page, and reflective page each ask for a different point of view, and that separation forced me to see gaps in my own understanding. If I could not explain the project clearly from the stakeholder perspective or from the developer perspective, then the project itself was not yet clear enough.

That experience showed me how much communication is part of technical work. Writing about artifacts, describing design changes, and organizing the portfolio required me to connect decisions to reasons. In that sense, the portfolio was not separate from the capstone. It was another stage of learning, because explanation turned vague effort into something more thoughtful and defensible.

Conclusion

The capstone changed the way I think about growth in software projects. I finished the quarter with a stronger understanding that good work is rarely produced in one clean pass. It develops through feedback, revision, documentation, and the discipline to keep improving the project after the first idea stops feeling new.

The next step for this capstone is to keep refining the artifacts and project explanation so the work better represents the full quarter of development. More broadly, I want to carry forward the habits that this project made visible: narrowing scope earlier, revising more willingly, and building steady progress instead of waiting for perfect momentum.
